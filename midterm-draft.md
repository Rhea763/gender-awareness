## Title

**Random Identity: A Short Interactive Experience About Everyday Gender Expectations**

Presenter: Yitong Zhou

Research Advisor: Lawted Wu

## Research Question & Hypothesis

**Research Question**

How does Random Identity affect the sense of unfairness for people who have not studied gender?

**Hypothesis**

I hypothesize that after using Random Identity, they will feel more that the same choice getting different feedback is unfair.

## Background

This project is about gender expectations in daily life.

Many people say they support gender equality. But in real situations, the same action can still be judged in different ways.

For example, before a public speech, one person may spend time on appearance. Another person may spend time practicing. One action may be called "professional" for one gender, but "not serious enough" for another gender.

This matters because these judgments are not only about one choice. They can shape confidence, stress, school life, work life, and how people explain failure to themselves.

Ordinary people should care because this is not only a debate in books. It can happen in small comments, advice from friends, classroom tasks, job interviews, and online life.

My project asks people to notice this problem through a small experience, not through a lecture.

## Literature Review

Eagly and Karau's role congruity theory says that bias can happen when a woman's gender role is seen as different from a leadership or professional role. Their work helps explain why the same confident action may be judged differently. What is still not solved is how ordinary people can notice this bias in a small daily scene. My project uses one same choice and two different feedback paths to make that gap easier to see.

Glick and Fiske's work on ambivalent sexism shows that sexism is not only hostile. It can also look kind, protective, or "for your own good." This is important for my project because many gender expectations do not sound like direct attacks. They can sound like advice. What is still not solved is how to show this without making users feel blamed. My entry point is to make the work exploratory and non-diagnostic.

Schmitt and Branscombe argue that seeing unfair treatment as discrimination can reduce self-blame. This connects directly to my ending message: the problem may not be that you did something wrong. What is still not solved is how a short interactive tool can support this shift. My project tests whether a simple comparison can make unfairness easier to name.

Bain's work on subtle sexism says that subtle gender bias is hard to explain. Because it is unclear, people may blame themselves. This is close to my main concern. My project focuses on the moment when a user may think, "I did not do anything wrong. Why is the world like this?"

Research on Chinese university students shows a mixed picture. Some studies suggest that open gender stereotypes are weaker now. Other studies still find implicit career stereotypes, the idea that men are more "brilliant," and biased explanations in math-related cases. This gap is important. It suggests that saying "I believe in equality" may not be the same as seeing bias in a real situation. My project works inside this gap.

Kiddo, Fair Play, and IDEM show that games and interactive stories can help people notice bias. But each has limits for my topic. Kiddo is for children. Fair Play is about race, not gender, and it often needs guided discussion. IDEM is mainly a workplace training product and lacks strong public research evidence. My project is different because it is light, browser-based, and built around the same-choice, different-feedback comparison.

## Research Design / Method

My artifact is **Random Identity**, a short browser-based interactive experience.

The current MVP is one single web page. It takes about two to three minutes. The user first reads a simple public speech scenario. They choose between spending time on appearance or spending time practicing.

After the choice, the user sees feedback from one path first. Then the same choice is shown again in another path. The user is not told the gender at the start. At the comparison page, they see that the same choice got different feedback.

The main target users are people who have not studied gender. They can be classmates, friends, or people from social media. This is a convenience sample, so I will not claim that the result represents all people.

The feedback data will be simple. Inside the page, I ask one required question: "When the two results are different, which reaction is closer to yours?" The options are: strange or unfair, normal, or not sure.

I also ask one optional open question: "What does this make you think of in real life?" Offline, I plan to ask a few follow-up questions: Which step felt strange? When did you notice the difference? Did it feel real?

For analysis, I will not make strong causal claims. I will count the answer choices and read the open responses for common themes. I will look for words about unfairness, self-blame, social expectations, and real-life examples.

## Research Plan & Challenges

**Current progress**

The research question and hypothesis are now fixed. I have built the MVP page. I have also collected literature on role congruity, ambivalent sexism, subtle sexism, attribution, Chinese gender attitudes, and existing intervention games.

**Plan**

June 14 to June 16: clean the English presentation, check the literature list, and make sure the demo is stable.

June 17 to June 19: rehearse the seven-minute talk, prepare backup answers, and improve the wording of the user study plan.

June 20: present the midterm defense.

After the midterm: run the small user study, collect feedback, analyze the answers, and decide whether the next version should add more scenes, a guide character, or a hidden refusal choice.

**Challenges**

The first challenge is scope. The full idea has more scenes, a guide character, hidden refusal, and data logs. But the MVP must stay small enough to test.

The second challenge is measurement. A short experience may affect attitude, but it may not change behavior. So I will focus on the sense of unfairness first.

The third challenge is ethics. I do not want users to feel diagnosed or blamed. The experience should feel safe, anonymous, and optional.

The fourth challenge is evidence. My study will use a small convenience sample. I need to be honest about that limit.

## Expected Results — user study not yet run

The user study has not been run yet, so I do not have results.

I expect that some users will notice the unfairness more clearly after seeing the two paths side by side.

I expect that some users will connect the scene to small moments in school, work, family, or online life.

I also expect that not everyone will react the same way. Some users may think the feedback is normal. Some may be unsure. These reactions are also useful, because they show where the design needs clearer framing.

My expected result is not that one short game can solve gender bias. My expected result is smaller: the game may help users notice a double standard and say why it feels unfair.
